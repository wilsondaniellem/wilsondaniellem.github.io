Long Run Regional Development After 19th Century Rail Expanison in Mexico

Rail Expansion and the Distirbution of Economic Activity

This study explores whether rapid rail expansion during the Porfiriato, the dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz from 1876 to 1911, permanently changed the spatial distribution of economic activity. Infrastructure is a sunk investment that can create geographically favorable conditions in connected regions and permanently change the prevalence of economic activity. For this study I digitize historic rail maps from 1882 – 1916 and census population data from 1901 to 1940. Following the empirical strategy of Jedwab and Moradi (2016) , I first determine whether a new spatial distribution arose after rail expansion, and whether this distribution was persistent. Persistence will be tested using two negative historic shocks:partial destruction of rail lines during the Revolution in the 1910s and privatization of the network in the 1990s. Continued economic activity in regions initially connected to rail, despite prolonged service disruptions, suggest that 19th century infrastructure investments may have had a permanent, path dependent effects on regional development.
